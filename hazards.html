HAZARDS

Risks and dangers commonly faced in mountaineering.

Extreme Weather Conditions
Mountains are known for sudden and unpredictable weather changes. Strong winds, heavy snowfall, and rapid temperature drops can occur without warning, making climbs more dangerous and reducing visibility.
Avalanches
Avalanches are one of the most serious hazards in mountaineering. They occur when large masses of snow slide down slopes, often triggered by weather conditions or human movement.
Altitude-Related Illnesses
High-altitude environments can cause health issues such as altitude sickness due to reduced oxygen levels. Symptoms may include headaches, dizziness, and fatigue, affecting a climber's ability to continue safely.
Crevasses and Glaciers
Glaciers often contain deep cracks known as crevasses, which may be hidden under snow. Falling into a crevasse can be life-threatening without proper equipment and awareness.
Falling Rocks and Ice
Loose rocks and falling ice are common hazards in mountainous regions, especially during warmer conditions. These falling objects can cause serious injuries to climbers below.
Physical Exhaustion and Fatigue

Mountaineering demands high levels of physical endurance. Long climbs, heavy equipment, and extreme conditions can lead to exhaustion, increasing the risk of accidents and poor decision-making.
